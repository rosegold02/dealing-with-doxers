# Dealing with Doxers

This guide is for artists or commissioners of adult content who want to avoid being doxed or limit the damage doxing causes them.

**If you are viewing this guide because you got doxed, skip to Section 3.**

## 1. Anatomy of a Dox

Doxers want to fill you and the people you care about with rage, fear, and panic. To blunt or deflect attacks, you must understand how doxers operate.

### 1A. Target Identification

The first thing a doxer will do is identify someone they want to harass. Though some pretend to abide by a code, the reality is that anyone can become a target at any time, even would-be doxers. Let's assume, then, that you are the target.

Once a doxer targets you, they will usually archive your posts so that they can still harass you even if you delete them later.

### 1B. Attack Surface

The doxer first collects all publicly available information directly attached to your account. This includes obvious ties, like linking your Telegram or Discord to your DeviantArt account, but also ones you might not expect.

Doxers can use tools like [Sherlock](https://github.com/sherlock-project/sherlock) to identify accounts online that share the same username (e.g. your Reddit, Github, Youtube, Cashapp, etc.).

If you have a public Paypal.me link, Cashapp, or Ko-fi tip jar, doxers can use them to learn your legal name and/or email address. If you're an artist, some doxers will commission you directly in a way that reveals your personal information without revealing theirs (e.g. by using CashApp). 

Doxers can also use the [Wayback Machine](https://web.archive.org/) to look at archived versions of your personal sites, which sometimes reveal personal information or ties to other accounts, especially on old sites like DeviantArt, FurAffinity, and Tumblr.

If you post publicly about your birthday, doxers can use it to learn your age. "Wow, I can't believe I'm 25 already!" to a doxer means DOB. 7/17/01.

Last but not least, any public posts they find will be checked for clues, especially your Reddit and Twitter acounts. If that cute photo of your cat includes a blurry Amazon shipping label in the background, you might have done the doxer's hard work for them. As well, "Just landed at sunny Tijuana Mexico, baby!" lets doxers look up flights to your destination at the date of your post and narrow down the departure cities, one of which is likely your home.

### 1C. Data Mining

If the doxer hasn't yet identified your legal name, they will go to a site like [breach.vip](breach.vip) to cross-reference the data they've gathered already with data that's been leaked over the years. 

Even if you practice good internet hygiene today, that won't help you if you gave Twitter your legal name when you signed up to the handle @FurryAnimeArtist over a decade ago. A Twitter data breach now permanently links those identities, letting doxers use them to identify you.

Doxers search the data breach space, identifying new usernames, emails, and passwords associated with your online identity. They use this to build a web that leads from your exclusively-online identity to your exclusively-offline identity. If you use the internet like a normal person, this process will usually be enough to reveal your legal name.

Data breaches often reveal your **passwords,** and if you reuse leaked passwords, doxers will log in to your account and deface it.

Data breaches can also reveal IP addresses associated with your account. Doxers can use these to identify the city you live in.

> **IP Grabbers:** If a doxer can't identify your IP address from breaches, they may send you a poisoned link that leaks your IP back to them when you click it.

### 1D. People Search
Having your legal name lets doxers learn your address and phone number through the use of people search sites likes [whitepages.com](https://www.whitepages.com/). These are most effective if you live in the United States, where data protection laws are generally weak. Though similar sites exist for other countries, doxers will sometimes give up on learning your address if they find that you live outside the US.
People search sites vary in usefulness by the uniqueness of your legal name. Doxers will use your date of birth or home city, if known, to narrow down the results. These sites can also reveal the personal information of your relatives, which doxers can use to spread the impact of their harassment.

> **Workplace:** If you have a LinkedIn account that shares either a particularly unique legal name or a username tied to your online identity via breaches, doxers will use it to identify your current workplace and your face.

### 1E. Confirmation

Doxers will generally consider their work complete once they know your legal name, address, date of birth, and phone number, and they have a picture of your face.
While the steps above seem airtight, doxers often make mistakes. For example, if you and another person happen to use the same username on two different sites, doxers may link you together by accident. The data available on free people search sites is often outdated, too. So, the doxer will test the accuracy of their dox.
One way they can do this is simply to post what they believe to be your dox in your discord or DMs and see if you have a strong reaction to it. If you react with confusion or don't react at all, then the doxer may be thrown off and wonder if they got your dox wrong. If you react with panic or rage, they will believe they got it right.
If you livestream, they may order a pizza or taxi to your house while you're streaming. If they hear a doorbell or a car honking over your livestream, they've confirmed your dox.

### 1F. Harassment
At this stage, the doxer will post your dox publicly and invite others to harass you. If your art is illegal where you live, they'll try to have you arrested. If you have a normal job, they'll contact your boss and try to get you fired. They'll try to contact your parents and tattle about your art. They'll sign your emails up to spam newsletters, flood your phone with calls, and order pizza to your house with payment due on delivery.

Usually this lasts until a few days after you stop reacting to it. They then archive the thread and your dox to their wiki, identify a new target, and start the process over.

## 2. Protecting Yourself from Doxers

The following steps will make you a much harder target for doxers, greatly reducing the likelihood that you get doxed.

Treat these steps like exercise: If you try to run a marathon day one, you'll burn out or hurt yourself. Each subsection orders its recommendations from most to least impactful.

### 2A. Become Untargetable

Artists rely on exposure and traction to build popularity and advertise their services, so anonymity is rarely an option for them.

However, if you are a commissioner, the simplest way to avoid being a target is to ask artists to not attribute your name to the works you commission. If doxers don't learn at least one username, they have absolutely nothing to work with. 

If you like, you can also use unique usernames for each platform on which you contact artists (FurAffinity, Twitter, etc.). If an artist forgets to credit you as "anonymous", then doxers will still have nothing useful.
If you still want to post your art to a gallery site, make sure you don't use any username, email, or password that could be linked to your real-life identity should all that information become public.

### 2B. Reducing Your Attack Surface
If you're an artist, the best way to mitigate your risk is to limit what information your payment methods reveal about you. Many artists have been doxed because Paypal or Cashapp leaked their legal name.

- If you use Paypal, you should set up a [Paypal Business account](https://www.paypal.com/us/business/open-business-account) and handle all transactions by invoice. This means giving Paypal more information about yourself, but in exchange, it does not reveal your personal information every time you send or accept a payment. It can be the difference between a doxer learning that your name is "John Robinson" or "FurryAnimeComms". You can use your business account for your Paypal.me link for a similar advantage.
- Use a dedicated email address for all accounts tied to your finances, and especially Paypal. In general, when creating an email account, make sure it doesn't resemble any of your usernames, as doxers can easily work backward from FurryAnimeArtist to furryanimeartist@gmail.com.
- If you use CashApp, use a unique username and a fake display name.
If your web pages are visible on the Internet Archive, you can request that they be removed by following the instructions [here](https://help.archive.org/help/how-do-i-request-to-remove-something-from-archive-org/).
Don't post about your age or birthday. If you can, delete all prior mentions of your birthday. When asked to indicate your birthday on sites, choose a random day on a random year a few years greater or lower than your actual birth year.
Finally, be careful what you post online, *especially photos,* and consider how that content can be used to identify you. 

### 2C. Empty the Mines

**Do not reuse passwords.** Browsers have good password managers these days, though you can also use [KeePass2](https://keepass.info/download.html), which has support for cloud synchronization while keeping your passwords safely encrypted.

Assume that, at minimum, the username, email address, and password you use to sign up to a website will become public *someday.* Close any accounts you no longer use so that the sites (hopefully!) delete your data before they suffer an inevitable breach.

Use [breach.vip](breach.vip) to look up where your usernames, passwords, or emails have surfaced in data breaches. Completely abandon any usernames that you can tie to your IRL identity. This can be a painful process, especially if that username is tied to dozens of accounts, but it's absolutely necessary. Because doxers always start by identifying your online identity as the target, if they cannot tie your online identity to a given username, it's useless to them, even if that username technically still links to your legal name.

> **Rebranding:**If the compromised username is a large part of your brand as an artist, then archives of your username likely exist in prior works. Changing your usernames and taking down old artwork may still have an impact, but unless you're an active target, be methodical rather than hasty. If you act emotionally rather than logically out of fear, you hand doxers a free win without them needing to lift a finger.

Separate your online identity into its own email. You can use GMail or Outlook to get additional free accounts, but ideally use [ProtonMail](https://proton.me/mail) or [TutaMail](https://tuta.com/). 

Use unique usernames for any accounts you don't want explictly connected. Sharing a username between twitter.com/furryanimeartist and deviantart.com/furryanimeartist may make sense, but sharing it with your Steam account or Github likely doesn't. Make sure your usernames aren't derivative of each other, because if doxers notice a pattern (i.e. one username being FurryAnimeArtist1 and another being FurryAnimeArtist4), they can figure out your other usernames anyway. [Correct Horse Battery Staple](https://www.explainxkcd.com/wiki/index.php/936:_Password_Strength) works just as well for usernames as for passwords.

If you can afford it, use a VPN like [Mullvad](https://mullvad.net/en). This will protect you from IP grabbers and will reduce the risk that the next data breach leaks your IP.

### 2D. Shred the Phonebook

If you live in the United States, consult this list of [data brokers](https://github.com/yaelwrites/Big-Ass-Data-Broker-Opt-Out-List) for instructions on how to get your personal information delisted. You can opt out of them manually, or if you can afford it, you can sign up to [EasyOptOuts](https://easyoptouts.com/) for continuous data monitoring and suppression.

If you live outside the United States, try to look up data brokers in your country. If they have your information, you should be able to ask them to remove or hide it.

## 3. Handling Being Doxed

Following these steps will mitigate the damage that doxers can do.

### 3A. Don't Panic

The primary goal of doxers is to harass you and get you to react emotionally. Being doxed is scary and frustrating, and that human reaction works against you to prolong your torment. The more you lash out, the more you'll ultimately suffer. Once you've been doxed, the best way to fight back is to stay calm.

While it's unnerving and stressful to live knowing that doxers hold your personal information, almost no one but you and they will care. Unlike hate groups of decades past, doxers are geographically dispersed and politically depressed. If they don't get the reaction they want out of you, they'll move on.

Let's talk about a doomsday scenario that may be on your mind.

Doxers may report you to law enforcement. However, even if your art *is* illegal where you live, it's **exceedingly unlikely** for the police to follow up on an anonymous tip from a doxer over drawings. Doxers have documented dozens of *attempted* reports, but none have materialized into action by law enforcement—neither arrest, nor trial, nor conviction. If this tactic was at all effective, it would have shown results by now.

Should you nonetheless be the first, remember this advice: Don't let the police into your home unless they present a judicial warrant, and [don't talk to them](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=d-7o9xYp7eE) until you've hired a lawyer to represent you.

### 3B. Disrupt Confirmation

The biggest opportunity you have to derail a dox is during the confirmation process. If you suspect you've been doxed, do not post about it publicly or in any group chat or server that doxers may have infiltrated. 

At this stage, if you can resist the urge to block and report people who post your dox and react instead as though it was completely random information, you may get them to second-guess their findings. This can be tricky depending on the situation, but it's a good first instinct, and it can't hurt you—after all, you can't get it out of their hands anyway and you can't keep them from posting it.

Refuse any pizza deliveries you didn't order. If you can, have someone else open the door, or simply don't answer, because doxers occasionally follow-up with the driver to confirm if your physical description matches photos they believe depict you.

Don't accept calls from unknown numbers—or if you must, use [call screening](https://support.google.com/phoneapp/answer/9118387?hl=en) if available, and don't greet the caller with your name. As an alternative, smartphones also let you [only receive calls from contacts.](https://support.google.com/fi/answer/12982560?hl=en&co=GENIE.Platform%3DAndroid)

### 3C. Handle Everything in Private

When you find out you've been doxed, the best thing you can do is act in public as though nothing happened. If you don't let doxers influence you behavior, you starve them of the attention and the reaction they crave. 

If the doxers already feel your details are confirmed, it's safe to block them when they post your address on social media, but don't call them out or draw art that makes fun of them. If you need to vent, vent privately with long-time friends or relatives. Assume any posts to public groups are monitored and act accordingly.

If you suspect doxers will contact your boss or parents, you could reach out to them proactively and explain that you're being harassed by internet weirdos. Alternatively, you can ignore it, because it's not altogether unlikely that they don't receive the message or disregard it as a hoax. Unfortunately, there's no one-size-fits-all approach. Regardless of the consequences of these conversations, no matter how severe, do not post about them publicly.

It's at this stage that you should consider whether to take down the art you've posted or not. Doxers will count your takedown as a win, but that could be worth it if it makes dealing with your boss or your parents easier. You can also go private temporarily and come back after the doxers have lost interest.

### 3D. Delist and Mute

If your dox shows up in Google or Bing, you can request its removal from search results. 

- [Remove from Google](https://support.google.com/websearch/troubleshooter/3111061)
- [Remove from Bing](https://www.microsoft.com/en-ca/concern/bing)
Google also lets you deindex your dox proactively using their [Results About You](https://support.google.com/websearch/answer/12719076) service.
Taking these steps doesn't remove your dox from the internet, but it helps limit its reach so that people don't stumble upon it by accident. In the same way, most sites have rules against doxing; if you see your dox or someone else's, be sure to report it so the account posting it gets muted or removed.

Doxers sometimes refer people to your home address by advertising services, sales, and the like. The same rules for handling pizza delivery given in Section 3B apply here. If doxers list your home address as a fictitious business on Google Maps, you can ask for it to be removed by following these [steps.](https://support.google.com/maps/thread/334376818/address-being-used-to-advertise-without-permission-i-am-the-owner-of-the-property?hl=en)

### 3E. What you Can't Do
It's highly unlikely that you'll be able to convince the doxers themselves to remove your information by plea or by threat. You also can't get their sites taken down. Trying just feeds the trolls, so don't bother.

Trying to get revenge on doxers isn't worth the effort. Even if the stars align and you were able to not only identify one but get them in trouble with their parents or the law, the spectacle of it all would simply attract another ten or twenty to take their place. Your time is better spent pushing your representatives for improved data protection laws—and if that seems equally hopeless, then you understand just what I mean.
